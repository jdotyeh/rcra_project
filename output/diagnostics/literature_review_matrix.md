# Literature Review Matrix

Papers in `literature/`, chronological. Columns: overview; research question; importance; data sources; Biennial Report use; design; conceptual omissions.

## Stafford (2002) — "The Effect of Punishment on Firm Compliance with Hazardous Waste Regulations" — Journal of Environmental Economics and Management 44: 290-308

- **Overview:** Tests whether EPA's 1990 Revised RCRA Civil Penalty Policy (some penalties raised 10-20x) increased facility compliance. Raw RCRIS counts show detected violations rising after 1992, but once inspection targeting and censoring are modeled, compliance rose about 10-20% - small relative to the penalty increase. Inspection and compliance probabilities also vary strongly across EPA regions.
- **Research question:** Did the sharp 1991 increase in RCRA penalty levels deter violations, i.e., increase firm compliance with hazardous waste regulations?
- **Why it matters:** Direct test of Becker-style deterrence for hazardous waste. Shows raw detected-violation counts mislead: compliance is only observed when a facility is inspected, so violation trends confound enforcement effort with firm behavior. Informs how much compliance a penalty increase actually buys.
- **Data sources:** RCRIS (EPA's RCRA Information System): inspections and violations 1986-1995, facility level. Panel of 8,411 facilities that generated/managed hazardous waste continuously over the period (~40% of the 1995 LQG+TSDF universe, ~90% of waste managed). Excludes federal facilities and small quantity generators. Study stops at 1995 because the 1996 self-audit policy changes disclosure incentives.
- **Biennial Report use:** YES - two uses of the Biennial Reporting System (BRS): (1) sample construction - the 1989/1991/1993/1995 BRS cycles identify facilities that generated or managed waste in all four survey years, defining the stable facility universe; (2) covariates - quantities generated, received from off-site, and managed on-site. Even (non-report) years interpolated; pre-1989 set to 1989 values.
- **Research design:** Censored bivariate probit: joint Inspection and Violation equations with correlated errors; violation status observed only if inspected. Effect identified from pre/post penalty-policy change in the violation equation; region dummies; facility waste activity, age, permit status, prior inspections/violations as controls.
- **What the approach omits:** Compliance is inferred only from detected violations - changes in inspector stringency or detection technology are conflated with true compliance. Uses recommended penalty schedule, not penalties actually assessed or paid. Time-based identification absorbs any contemporaneous change (e.g., other 1991-era RCRA rule changes). Survivorship: only continuously operating facilities; SQGs, federal facilities, and non-notifying (illegal) handlers invisible. State-level penalty discretion not observed.

## Stafford (2003) — "Assessing the Effectiveness of State Regulation and Enforcement of Hazardous Waste" — Journal of Regulatory Economics 23(1): 27-41

- **Overview:** Asks which state hazardous waste regulations and policies promote facility compliance, using ~8,000 facilities in 1995. Voluntary pollution prevention programs are associated with fewer violations overall; strict liability and allocating more staff to regional offices with fewer Class 1 (serious) violations; state waste-program spending correlates positively with overall violation probability (interpreted as spending responding to noncompliance) but negatively with Class 1 violations.
- **Research question:** Do state-level regulatory choices - strict liability, voluntary/mandatory pollution prevention programs, program spending, staffing allocation, environmental indicators, sustainability planning - affect facility compliance with RCRA regulations?
- **Why it matters:** RCRA is built on state primacy: states run authorized programs and can exceed federal stringency, so regulation/enforcement varies across states. Identifies which state policy levers actually move compliance - direct input for state program design.
- **Data sources:** RCRIS via EPA Envirofacts: 1995 inspections and violations (all violations and Class 1) for 8,000+ facilities regulated at least 4 years. Facility waste-activity covariates from BRS. State policy and resource variables (liability regime, P2 programs, spending, employees) from state program sources.
- **Biennial Report use:** YES - facility-level covariates extracted from the Biennial Reporting System: quantity of waste generated, quantity received from other generators, plus permit status and combustion / land-disposal unit indicators. These proxy waste activity, compliance costs, and inspection-targeting risk.
- **Research design:** Censored bivariate probit (Inspection + Violation equations, correlated errors) on a single 1995 cross-section; two violation specifications (any violation; Class 1 only); facility-specific and state-specific regressors, regional dummies.
- **What the approach omits:** Author-acknowledged: no data on fines actually paid, so the expected-penalty channel is unmeasured; criminal sanctions ignored; 'regulatory dealing' (multi-media firm-regulator agreements) ignored. Additionally: one-year snapshot - no dynamics or policy-change identification; state policies (esp. spending) plausibly endogenous to noncompliance; compliance of never-inspected facilities unobservable; state policies measured as adoption dummies, not implementation intensity.

## Lynch, Stretesky & Burns (2004) — "Determinants of Environmental Law Violation Fines Against Petroleum Refineries: Race, Ethnicity, Income, and Aggregation Effects" — Society and Natural Resources 17: 333-347

- **Overview:** Asks whether monetary penalties against petroleum refineries for CAA/CWA/RCRA violations vary with the racial, ethnic, and income makeup of the surrounding community. 206 penalties (Apr 2001-Apr 2003) at 99 of 153 operable US refineries. Census-tract demographics show no relationship to penalty size; at ZIP level, refineries in Hispanic ZIPs receive ~95% smaller penalties and each $1k of ZIP median income raises penalties ~12%. Aggregation unit changes the answer.
- **Research question:** Do extralegal community characteristics (race, ethnicity, income) affect assessed fine amounts - and do conclusions depend on whether community is measured at census-tract or ZIP-code level?
- **Why it matters:** Extends environmental justice research from facility siting to enforcement outcomes: if fines are systematically lower in minority/poor areas, deterrence is weaker exactly where hazards concentrate, and agencies fail their EJ commitments. Also a methodological warning: the modifiable areal unit problem can reverse EJ findings.
- **Data sources:** Refinery universe from US DOE/EIA Petroleum Supply Annual 2001 (153 refineries). Penalties, compliance and enforcement history from EPA ECHO. Facility lat/long from ECHO geocoded in ArcView to 2000 Census tracts; demographics from 2000 Census SF3 (tract and ZIP). Company characteristics from Dun & Bradstreet Million Dollar Database; pollution prevention ranking from Environmental Defense (1999).
- **Biennial Report use:** NO - enforcement and penalty data come from ECHO; no Biennial Report / waste-quantity data used. Facility hazard scale enters only through company size and P2 ranking proxies.
- **Research design:** Cross-sectional. Bivariate comparisons of mean/median penalties (t-tests, Mann-Whitney U) across African American vs White, Hispanic vs non-Hispanic, low- vs high-income areas; then negative binomial regressions of penalty amount (OLS rejected for heteroscedasticity), run in parallel at tract and ZIP level with enforcement-history, case, company/facility, and political-economic controls.
- **What the approach omits:** Single industry over a two-year window - thin external validity. Penalty amount is observed but underlying violation severity only coarsely controlled, so lenient sanctioning cannot be fully separated from smaller offenses. Selection into detection and into penalized enforcement actions is unmodeled (who gets inspected/prosecuted at all). No waste-volume or release measure for the facility. Purely associational; no strategy for causal identification.

## Sjoberg & Xu (2018) — "An Empirical Study of US Environmental Federalism: RCRA Enforcement From 1998 to 2011" — Ecological Economics 147: 253-263

- **Overview:** Studies whether decentralizing RCRA enforcement to states changes state enforcement behavior, 1998-2011. Builds a continuous authorization index (share of authorizable RCRA rules a state holds). Evaluations and violations stay flat and fines fall over the period, but none of these move with marginal authorization - no evidence of a race to the bottom.
- **Research question:** Does transferring RCRA enforcement authority from EPA to states (state authorization) change enforcement outcomes: number of evaluations (inspections), detected violations, proposed monetary penalties, and the violation/evaluation ratio?
- **Why it matters:** Core environmental federalism question - fear that state control breeds leniency and interstate competition in laxity. Unlike the Reagan-era one-shot decentralization literature, exploits RCRA's ongoing, gradual, rule-by-rule authorization margin, and treats decentralization as continuous rather than binary.
- **Data sources:** Authorization: EPA State Authorization Tracking System (StATS), rules authorized per state per year -> index = authorized share of authorizable rules x100. Enforcement outcomes: RCRAInfo via ECHO (evaluations, violations, penalty amounts). Controls: League of Conservation Voters scores, state GDP per capita, number of firms, governor party.
- **Biennial Report use:** NO - uses RCRAInfo enforcement/compliance records via ECHO; the Biennial Report waste data are not part of the design.
- **Research design:** State-year panel (50 states x 14 years) fixed-effects regressions: state FE, year FE, state-specific linear time trends; contemporaneous and lagged authorization. Robustness: log outcomes, coastal-state interactions, GDP-quartile and LCV interactions, pre-2007 subsample.
- **What the approach omits:** State-level aggregation erases facility- and inspector-level heterogeneity (composition of inspected facilities can shift invisibly). Identifies only MARGINAL authorization - base programs were authorized in the 1980s, so the big decentralization step is outside the data. Authorization is endogenous (states choose to apply); FE + trends, not an instrument. All rules weighted equally. Fines are proposed, not collected. Measures enforcement behavior only - no environmental or compliance outcomes (releases, waste quantities, firm behavior). Cannot explain the secular decline in fines it documents.

## Ozymy & Jarrell (2021) — "Does the Criminal Enforcement of Federal Environmental Law Deter Environmental Crime? The Case of the U.S. RCRA" — Barry Environmental and Earth Law Journal 11: 65-88

- **Overview:** Catalogs every environmental criminal prosecution from EPA Criminal Investigation Division cases FY1983-2019 (2,588 cases), of which 396 have RCRA as a primary charging statute (~15%). Prosecutions peak at 23/yr (1999) and fall to low single digits by the late 2010s. Estimated annual probability that a regulated facility is criminally prosecuted is roughly 0.1-0.7% (lower for corporate defendants). Concludes the criminal regime's deterrent capacity is limited.
- **Research question:** Does the federal criminal enforcement apparatus (EPA-CID investigation -> DOJ prosecution) plausibly deter RCRA hazardous waste crime, judged by detection and prosecution probabilities and penalty severity?
- **Why it matters:** Criminal prosecution is the apex sanction for the worst willful/knowing violations, yet its deterrent value was essentially untested. Grounds resource-policy arguments: EPA-CID staffed far below its statutory minimum of 200 agents; DOJ-ECS ~43 prosecutors.
- **Data sources:** Content analysis of EPA's public Summary of Criminal Prosecutions Database, FY1983-2019: all 2,588 case summaries coded (narrative, fiscal year, docket, state, defendant count, corporate defendant present); two coders, intercoder reliability ~95%. Regulated-universe denominator from ECHO (average 3,112 RCRA facilities, 2006-19, projected backward) and TRI.
- **Biennial Report use:** NO - prosecution summaries plus ECHO facility counts; Biennial Report not used (a BR-based generator universe would in fact give a much larger denominator).
- **Research design:** Descriptive quantitative content analysis: prosecution counts over time, by state, by defendant type; large-penalty case inventory; deterrence assessed by dividing annual prosecutions by the estimated facility universe to get prosecution probabilities.
- **What the approach omits:** No counterfactual or behavioral outcome - deterrence is inferred from low sanction probability, never from observed violation responses. Denominator problems: assumes 2006-19 ECHO facility counts (~3,112) hold back to 1983 and covers only that permitted universe - generators, transporters, non-permitted operators, and midnight dumpers are outside it, and the true offender pool is unknowable. Cases absent from EPA's database are invisible. State criminal prosecutions excluded. Perceived (rather than actual) sanction risk - what actually drives deterrence - is unmeasured.

## Guignet & Nolte (2024) — "Hazardous Waste and Home Values: An Analysis of Treatment and Disposal Sites in the United States" — JAERE 11(2): 487-521

- **Overview:** First nationwide hedonic property-value study of RCRA TSDFs: 2,389 TSDFs, 9.7M single-family transactions 2000-2018. Contamination discovery / corrective action (CA) opening associated with up to -5% for homes within 750m (mixed evidence); CA completion with +6-7% appreciation. Total capitalization ~$295M across the 195 TSDFs remediated since RCRA's inception; average lower-bound ex post benefit ~$14k per household.
- **Research question:** (i) Is TSDF proximity associated with lower home values? (ii) Does contamination discovery/CA opening depress nearby values? (iii) Does cleanup completion raise them? (iv) What are household welfare implications?
- **Why it matters:** RCRA corrective action benefits go essentially unmonetized in EPA benefit-cost analyses (only avoided costs counted); 12% of the US population lives within a mile of a CA site. Supplies the missing national benefit estimates and a template for welfare-interpretable hedonics (Banzhaf 2021 lower bound).
- **Data sources:** RCRAInfo Public Extract: 129,670 RCRA sites; TSDF universe (2,389 contiguous-US) and CA events - opening = earliest of event codes CA060/CA100/CA200, completion = CA999*/CA900*; 689 TSDFs with contamination CAs, 195 completed by 2018. Coordinates from EPA Federal Registry Service. Prices: Zillow ZTRAX (9.7M transactions) with parcel/spatial cleaning via PLACES (Boston U.). Census TIGER geographies.
- **Biennial Report use:** NO - facility universe and cleanup timeline come from RCRAInfo handler and Corrective Action modules; Biennial Report waste quantities not used (site hazard intensity is not measured).
- **Research design:** Quasi-experimental hedonics: spatial difference-in-differences and triple differences around CA opening and completion; treated = homes near CA TSDFs, controls = homes near the 1,700 never-contaminated TSDFs and farther distance rings; extensive fixed effects; ex post welfare via Banzhaf (2021) lower-bound capitalization.
- **What the approach omits:** Welfare counts only owner-occupied single-family homes - renters and multifamily housing (denser, urban, often lower-income populations) excluded. Capitalization is a lower-bound, partial measure: health, ecological, groundwater, and non-resident benefits omitted. Requires residents to perceive CA milestones - information diffusion unobserved; price response conflates perceived and actual risk. RCRAInfo CA event dates missing/inconsistent for some sites. Averages mask heterogeneity in contamination severity and exposure pathways.

## Brodin & Guignet (2024) — "Layers of Injustice: A Distributional Assessment of Toxic Chemical Facilities, Releases, and Cleanups" — Journal of Environmental Management 370: 122415

- **Overview:** Environmental justice assessment across four 'layers' of the RCRA pipeline: facility siting, occurrence of corrective actions (CAs), CA duration, and permanence of remediation. 41,361 LQGs/TSDFs; 199 contamination CAs initiated 2000-2019. RCRA sites are more likely in tracts with higher nonwhite/Hispanic shares; conditional on a site, CAs are LESS likely in minority tracts but more likely where income is lower; cleanups take significantly longer in nonwhite/Hispanic tracts; remedy permanence shows no robust disparity.
- **Research question:** Are (i) siting of RCRA facilities, (ii) occurrence of releases/CAs, (iii) duration of investigation and cleanup, and (iv) implementation of less-permanent remedies distributed inequitably across race, ethnicity, and income?
- **Why it matters:** Moves EJ analysis beyond siting to the full regulatory life cycle of active industrial facilities (vs the Superfund-centric literature); ~113M Americans live within 3 miles of a RCRA CA site; directly relevant to federal mandates for distributional analysis of regulations.
- **Data sources:** US Census 2000 Decennial (SF) tract data - 63,083 contiguous-US tracts (% nonwhite, % Hispanic, % poverty, median income, density) - GIS-merged (TIGER/Line) with RCRAInfo Public Extract: 129,670 sites filtered to 41,361 LQG and/or TSDF facilities; CA module event codes define CA opening (earliest CA060/CA100/CA200), completion, and remedy type; NAICS industry codes.
- **Biennial Report use:** NO - facility classifications (LQG/TSDF status) and CA events come from RCRAInfo handler/CA modules; Biennial Report quantities not used, so 'risk' is a coarse flag rather than measured waste volume.
- **Research design:** Four nested models: probit of a tract containing a RCRA site (63,083 tracts); probit of CA occurrence (41,361 facilities); Cox proportional hazards of CA duration with censoring retained (196 CAs, 77 completed); probit of permanent vs containment remedy (77 completed CAs). Facility-type, risk-flag, and industry controls.
- **What the approach omits:** Authors flag: causality ambiguous, coarse broad-brush averages. Conceptually: demographics measured in 2000 while facilities were sited decades earlier - cannot separate discriminatory siting from post-siting demographic sorting (move-in). CA occurrence conflates true release rates with detection/reporting intensity - under-detection in minority tracts is observationally identical to lower risk. Tract-level cross-section misses within-tract gradients and temporal change. Duration/permanence samples tiny (196/77). Omitted population dimensions (linguistic isolation, age, immigration status).
